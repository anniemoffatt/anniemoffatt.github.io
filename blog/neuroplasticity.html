Neuroplasticity

Understanding the Benefits

The concept of neuroplasticity and the idea that our brains are pliable is really reassuring.

Knowing that we can change and adapt at any age brings comfort and a sense of hope/optimism - both of which are super important when it comes to learning new things and persevering through the hard parts.

Ways to Increase Neuroplasticity

The AGES method offers a useful framework for improving neuroplasticity:

  • Attention - Minimise distractions to enhance focus
  • Generation - Create meaningful connections by associating new information with prior knowledge
  • Emotion - Engage with material emotionally to reinforce learning
  • Spacing - Space out learning to avoid overload and improve retention

Engaging

I plan on incorporating the AGES method into my learning by practising the following:

  • Attention - I live in a busy household with lots of distractions, I'm working on setting and sticking to regular non negotiable 'work' hours.
  • Generation - I'm not sure I fully get this one - but I've found that by learning new things during the course I better understand previous work situations. The developers I used to work with might say things that at the time made no sense - but I've found myself thinking 'oh that's what they meant!'. This has also helped me visualise real life situations where course material might be used.
  • Emotion - I really enjoy the rush you get when you finally understand something complicated - I'm trying to remind myself of that feeling when something feels overwhelming.
  • Spacing - I think the spacing is mostly done for us during Foundations. The curriculum is divided over weeks and doesn't dive too deep into any one subject at a time.

Growth Mindset

What Is It?

A growth mindset is believing that ability and intelligence can develop over time through effort, perseverance and engagement. It's the opposite of a fixed mindset where people think their abilities are static/can't be changed significantly post childhood.

What Surprised Me

One resource talked about changing 'fail' grades (I'm assuming such as 'Not Achieved' or 'Failed') to 'Not Yet'. My last role was at a university and I worked on a project that followed the same concept - we changed certain grades to 'softer' ones. Admittedly at the time I didn't really see the point, they're all just variations of the same message. But I can see now, from the evidence, just how effective this shift can be.

How I'll Use This

The same resource mentioned someone who felt they had 'wasted' most of their life by having a fixed mindset - that's a pretty good deterrent in itself.

The video also talks about educators creating growth mindset classrooms - so I think the nature of the course itself helps here. By not 'scoring' us it takes away the need for validation which I've actually found really nice. Getting a bad grade can make you feel you're not capable/in the wrong place which I think could really throw someone off here.

The way I can integrate this into my own learning I think is to just trust the process, and focus on effort, perseverance and engagement.
